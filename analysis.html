Computational Analysis of Text

One of the first questions posed after reading the series is: what gender does Nelson incorporate the most dialogue from throughout the series? Nelson's focus in writing the articles is the working woman, with an emphasis on women. Did Nelson choose to expose their plight most by giving them a direct voice, or by representing the male voice as an oppressing force? We found that the female voice is significantly more represented than the male voice. This is what we expected; Nelson tends to interview women of all classes and working conditions. When interviewing both a male and female in a similar working environment, her articles include significantly more dialogue from the women workers. Here, we can see that the female voice is represented nearly twice as much as the male voice.

After it was determined that Nelson allowed for significantly more female dialogue, we wanted to see how this broke down when looking at the types of people getting a voice in the series. Nelson spoke with many different types of people in her expos√©. We found that overall, the working girl has the most amount of dialogue. Even when compared against Nelson's own dialogue, the working girl is represented nearly twice as much as the author. This corresponds to our findings in the gender bar graph above; the female voice is most represented. Furthermore, the voice of the employee when considered with the voice of the working girl, is over 50% of total dialogue; therefore, it is significantly more represented than that of the employer even when including the foreperson.

Explanation of Project Archetypes

We started by cataloging speakers in relation to their employment. The working girl, because of the focus of the series, received her own tag. Employers were marked as owners of establishments (including familial ties). When an employee was recognized as a foreperson or someone with rank over other employees they were marked foreperson. An employee was any person employed by an establishment that could not be catergorized as a working girl or foreperson. Nell Nelson, being the series' author, received her own tag. Messenger was an otherwise unlabeled character who spoke on behalf of another. Prominent Males and Prominent Females were characters who were of higher social standing than the working girls and who do not fall into any other archetype. The benefactor was a male character who was the focus of a lengthy back and forth with Nelson in the August 2nd article. In several articles another reporter (not Nelson) spoke; therefore, that speaker recieved the reporter tag. Also, some articles incorporated the investigations of a Health Department Inspector, who was marked inspector. Finally, unidentified was used to represent speaking characters when identity was unknown or unclear.


Once we had an idea of how the dialogue was distributed, we then wanted to see how the different groups of people in the text were taking ownership of not only the material things around them, but also other people. We hypothesized that the employer would frequently take ownership of those in his employ; however, the network analyses indicate even more interesting relationships of ownership than what was expected. We created a network analysis for every article individually as well as one including all of the articles.
